Information

Chris Corson-Scott was born in 1985 in Auckland, New Zealand, where he currently lives and works.

He typically works with 8x10 and 4x5 inch large-format view cameras, and always uses film. Post production involves scanning the film to facilitate digital retouching and colour-correction, before a ‘c-print’ or ‘pigment print’ is produced.

The subjects are usually close to home, and often the sites of personal memories, particularly those of childhood. When the works include people, they are either close friends participating in the construction of the image, or anonymous members of the public, unaware of the camera. In the first respect, this bears a strong affinity to the subjects of English landscape and French Impressionist painting, and in the second to the Neo-Realist cinema of Italy, or the films of Yasujiro Ozu, Andrei Tarkovsky and Abbas Kiarostami.

Corson-Scott attempts to absorb the lessons of painters such as Paul Cezanne and Jackson Pollock. Where most photographs tend to isolate a single subject against a background, he seeks a consistent intensity of vision, both conceptually and spatially, across the whole picture surface. The photographs seek to organise and condense the peculiarities of the contemporary everyday into a timeless image.
